## 1. The problem we found

Working through Ashley's real estimate (6BR/7BA, 5,000 sqft, ~$30M, downsizing+move)
surfaced four separate flaws that compounded into a $26K / **16-working-day** quote:

1. **Per-room hours were too high.** A primary bath showed 1.7 concierge + 2.4
   specialist hours (4.1 combined) for linens, toiletries, and meds.
2. **The role split was backwards in personal rooms.** Bathrooms, bedrooms, and
   closets carried large *specialist* allocations — a laborer going through the
   client's medicine cabinet and wardrobe.
3. **Property value inflated hours** (×1.5 on a trophy estate), stacking on the
   square-footage multiplier (×1.25) to nearly double the concierge hours — which
   is what pushed the calendar to 16 days and made margins look enormous.
4. **The calendar assumed one concierge doing every hour alone**, with no way to
   reflect how the job is actually staffed.

---

## 2. Core principles (the fix)

1. **Every room is a mix of two kinds of work:**
   - **Valuables / decisions / personal** → the **Transition Concierge** (sorting
     keep/donate/sell, packing fine china, flagging silver/art for appraisal,
     personal items, documentation).
   - **Bulk / muscle / junk** → the **Property Specialist** (moving furniture,
     hauling, staging donations for Goodwill).
   - The *ratio* is a property of the room. A bathroom is almost all concierge.
     A garage is almost all muscle. A kitchen depends on how fine the contents are.

2. **Property value drives PRICE, not hours.** A trophy estate doesn't take twice
   the hours — it commands a premium *fixed price*. Value is removed from the hours
   math entirely (kills the double-count with room scoring + Collections).

3. **The "premium estate" flag shifts valuables rooms toward the concierge.** In a
   $30M home the kitchen *is* fine china, the dining room *is* silver, the living
   room *is* art — so more of that work routes to the concierge and to Collections,
   not to a laborer.

4. **Service type reflects documentation burden.** A living owner directs decisions
   (fast). A deceased owner means the concierge documents every item (slow). This is
   a concierge-hours factor, not a blanket multiplier.

5. **One concierge owns the job, on-site throughout.** Days = concierge hours ÷ 7.
   Property specialists are the crew that compresses the *physical* work; they never
   become a second project manager.

6. **Honest cost is separated from client price.** See §5.

## 4. Three worked scenarios

Calendar = concierge hours ÷ 7 (one concierge, on-site throughout). Labor is at the
**current standard billable rates** ($150 concierge / $100 specialist) — this is the
honest internal basis, **not** the client price (see §5).

| Scenario | Concierge hrs | Specialist hrs | Labor @ $150/$100 | Days |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A. Standard downsizing** — 3BR/2BA, 3,000 sqft, living owner | 11 | 12 | $2,850 | 2 |
| **B. Trophy estate downsizing+move** — 6BR/7BA, 5,000 sqft, $30M, premium | 46 | 23 | $9,200 | 7 |
| **C. Estate settlement** — same estate, deceased, documentation | 42 | 27 | $9,000 | 6 |

**What changed for the trophy estate (B):** the old engine produced **$26K / 16
days**. Honest hours produce **$9,200 / 7 days** — one concierge, believable
calendar, no laborer in anyone's closet. The gap between that $9.2K and what the
client pays is now an explicit pricing decision, not buried in fake hours (see §5).

## 5. Pricing structure — cost vs. price (the fixed-price model)

The app stops pretending the price *is* the hours. Four layers:

| Layer | Who sees it | Scenario B example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Estimated labor** (hours × billable rate) | internal | $9,200 |
| **2. Contingency buffer** (+20%, fixed-price risk) | internal | ~$11,040 floor |
| **3. Client fixed price** (anchored to market) | **client** | **[YOU SET] — e.g. $25–30K** |
| **4. Margin** (price − true payroll cost) | internal | [YOU SET payroll] |

- The client sees **one fixed number and a scope** — never an hourly rate. This
  resolves the "$315/hr is unjustifiable" problem: there is no disclosed hourly.
- The buffer protects you on fixed-price overruns.
- Margin becomes **visible information you manage**, not an accident. If a $28K
  quote sits on $9.2K of labor, you *want* to see that.
